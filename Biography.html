Sir Tim Berners-Lee

Sir Tim Berners-Lee is a British computer Scientist who is credited with inventing what we know today as the World Wide Web (Brooker 2018). However, before becoming the known figure that he is today, Sir Berners-Lee studied physics at The University of Oxford and then went on to be a software engineer. It is also worth noting that both his parents had worked on the Ferranti Mark I (the first commercially available computer) and computing came naturally to him (Akerman 2016). After graduating from Oxford University, Berners-Lee worked for companies such as Plessey Telecommunications and CERN, the latter at which he developed the code for what we know today as the World Wide Web. This was done using a neXT computer which was a product of the late Steve Jobs (Arthur 2011). Rather primitively this computer had a red hand written label stuck to it which warned, “This machine is a server. DO NOT POWER IT DOWN” (A short history of the Web | CERN. [no date]). Though this was the WWW in its infancy it was not available to the public. This later changed in 1991 where the web came online in America. A few years later, Berners-Lee left CERN and joined MIT where he founded W3C and at this point there were more than 10,000 servers and 10 million users around the world (A short history of the Web | CERN. [no date]). He has gone on to receive numerous honours throughout his career including named one of the 20th century’s most influential men, the Charles Stark Draper prize and his knighthood (Dennis 2022).

Though Sir Tim Berners-Lee is already an incredibly accomplished Computer Scientist (among other things), he is not quite ready to hang up his boots and has been working on a data protection tool. This programme is called Solid which is aimed at giving individuals control of their data once again. This is done by allowing the user to control access their data and storing it in “decentralised Pods” (About Solid · Solid. [no date]). It is no secret that as users of the internet, we are all monitored and our data is sold to large corporations like Facebook, Amazon, Google and even government agencies (Brooker 2018). With billions of data on individuals, these organisations can deliberately influence things like elections, political and social behaviours to their benefit in unethical ways. Therefore Sir Tim Berners-Lee’s latest creation has the potential to be incredibly influential to the modern and future world we live in. What else can we expect from one of the most influential minds of the last century?
