Computational Thinking

Computational thinking is a process for solving problems through several steps and creating appropriate and effective solutions. The solution(s) should be clear and efficient enough to be carried out by a human, machine or computer. According to Jeanette Wing, this concept can not only be applied in computer science but even in everyday life (Grover and Pea 2013). This process is made up of 5 steps: Decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, generalisation and algorithm which all together creates a step by step method to finding solutions for problems (Hunsaker et al. 2018).

Decomposition requires the breaking down of a problem into smaller ones that should be more manageable. Therefore permitting smaller problems to be solved more easily which will ultimately contribute to the bigger, more difficult task.

Pattern recognition requires analysing the problem(s) to properly evaluate how to approach them. As the name states, pattern recognition allows us to recognise similarities in our problems. This could help eliminate multiple problems with one solution and even reuse ideas/code with slight modification. Therefore proving to aid efficiency with regards to time and/or resources.

Abstraction can be understood as simplifying the problem, reducing unnecessary information. In the words of (Wing 2017) “used to capture essential properties”. This means that one’s approach to problem solving through code or computation should be specific enough to solve the matter at hand.

Generalisation helps to create an adjustable solution that may boost adaptability for solving future problems, “to let one object stand for many” (Hunsaker et al. 2018) (Wing 2017). Therefore the solutions need to be applicable to the immediate task but also have scope to cover other problems that may pop up.

Algorithm is deploying step by step instructions that can be followed by a computer or person. This is where the solution is being described to solve the problem. For example having a system for adjusting the temperature in a room. Depending on what parameters are met, this will determine the action required for lowering or increasing the temperature.

Adapting computational thinking into work, academic and even daily tasks allows for efficient strategical problem solving. This philosophy has the potential to make academic assignments and work related tasks more manageable by breaking them down (decomposition) and even grouping them together (pattern recognition). It is also useful for making complicated, tasks to be simplified through abstraction and generalisation with the potential of using previously used solutions to make tasks less labour intensive. Finally it can help make developing step by step solutions (algorithms) clearer and easier to execute. By implementing computational thinking into my approach to computer programming, it will make me a more efficient problem solver, tackling complex tasks without being overwhelmed and within required time constraints.
